Understanding Pet Ownership in Geelong & Casey:

Geography, Breeds, Ages & Names


A visual exploration of pet populations and behavioural trends across Geelong and Casey.

This dashboard uses publicly available pet registration data to examine how pet ownership varies across the cities of Geelong and Casey. It highlights where pets are most concentrated, how dog–cat ratios differ by suburb, and how these patterns relate to age, breed and naming trends. Together, the visualisations offer a clear picture of the cultural and behavioural characteristics shaping pet ownership across both regions.

The dashboard begins by examining the geographic distribution of pet ownership across Geelong and Casey. Mapping pets per 1,000 residents highlights where pets form a larger part of household life and reveals how pet-keeping culture varies from suburb to suburb. The map below provides an overview of these spatial patterns before we explore age, breed and naming trends in later sections.

What This Map Shows

The choropleth map compares how common pet ownership is across Geelong and Casey by measuring registered pets per 1,000 residents. This standardised rate reveals genuine differences in pet-keeping culture rather than simply reflecting population size. Darker shading highlights suburbs where pets form a noticeably larger part of household life.

How To Read The Map

Hover over any suburb to view cat and dog registration counts as well as the estimated population. Comparing Geelong and Casey side-by-side reveals different spatial distributions: Casey suburbs tend to show stronger contrasts between high and low ownership regions, while Geelong displays a more gradual spatial gradient.

The first map shows where pet ownership is concentrated, but it treats all pets as equal. In reality, some suburbs are far more dog-dominated, while others lean strongly toward cats. This difference tells us about the kinds of households living in each area from family-oriented, dog-heavy suburbs to apartment-dense neighbourhoods with more cats.

The following diverging map visualises this balance directly, showing whether dogs or cats are more common in each suburb, and by how much.

Dog vs Cat Dominance by Suburb

This smaller map focuses on the balance between dogs and cats in each suburb. The diverging colour scale shows the log₁₀ ratio of registered dogs to registered cats:

  • Blue suburbs have relatively more cats than dogs.
  • Red suburbs have relatively more dogs than cats.
  • White / pale shades indicate a more balanced mix.
  • The more intense the shade is, the higher the ratio is respectively.

Because the ratio is shown on a log scale, extreme dog- or cat-dominated suburbs appear more intensely coloured, while balanced suburbs sit near the centre of the colour scale. This makes it easy to identify pockets of dog-leaning vs cat-leaning communities across both councils.


Why no blue suburbs appear: Blue indicates areas where cats outnumber dogs. Although the colour scale includes blue to represent the full possible range, none of the suburbs in Geelong or Casey fall below zero on the log₁₀(dogs/cats) scale. In other words, dogs outnumber cats in every suburb. The inclusion of blue in the legend reflects the complete scale used for the ratio, while its absence on the map highlights a genuine pattern in the data: across both regions, dog ownership is consistently more prevalent than cat ownership.

This ratio map complements the preceding population maps by showing not just where pet ownership is high, but which type of pet shapes each community. The following sections explore how these species differences relate to age profiles, breed popularity, and naming patterns.

Understanding Suburb-level Patterns in Context:

While the ratio map highlights how dog- and cat-dominance varies across individual suburbs, it is also important to interpret these patterns at a broader scale. The following charts step back from suburb-level detail to compare overall pet ownership between the two councils and to identify which suburbs have the highest concentration of pets per resident. Together, these views help contextualise the earlier maps and offer clearer insight into how local variations fit within the wider demographic landscape of Geelong and Casey.

City-level Dog vs Cat Ownership

This chart compares the total number of registered dogs and cats across the Cities of Geelong and Casey. It highlights the broader city-level differences in pet ownership and provides essential context for interpreting the suburb-level trends shown in earlier maps. Overall, both Geelong and Casey register substantially more dogs than cats, reinforcing the consistent dog-dominance seen across almost all suburbs in both councils.

Top 10 Suburbs

This chart identifies the suburbs with the highest number of registered pets per 1,000 residents across both councils. Standardising by population makes it possible to pinpoint areas where pet ownership is particularly concentrated, regardless of suburb size.A notable pattern emerges: 8 out of the top 10 suburbs are located in Geelong, suggesting a higher density of pet-owning households compared to Casey and reflecting regional differences in lifestyle and housing patterns.


Taken together, the suburb-level maps and city comparisons paint a clear picture of pet ownership across Geelong and Casey: both councils show strong dog dominance, and pet ownership is most concentrated in a small number of Geelong suburbs. These patterns reflect broader differences in population density, housing types and lifestyle preferences across the two regions. Having explored where pets are most common and how dogs and cats differ geographically, the dashboard now shifts focus to who these pets are: their ages, breeds and naming trends.

Pet Characteristics & Behavioural Patterns

The earlier visualisations focus on where pet ownership is concentrated across Geelong and Casey, and how the balance between dogs and cats varies by suburb. This next section shifts attention from location to who these pets are, exploring demographic and behavioural patterns such as age, breed popularity, and naming trends.

Only the Geelong dataset contains detailed, pet-level records (including age, breed, and name). For this reason, the following charts draw on Geelong’s data to provide a closer look at the characteristics and preferences of pet-owning households in that region.

Age Profile of Registered Pets

This chart shows how pet ages are distributed in Geelong. Use the dropdown to compare all pets, dogs only and cats only, revealing how longevity and registration behaviour differ between species.

Most pets are between 4 and 10 years old, with dogs clustering around 6–9 years. Cats have a slightly broader spread and appear more often in older age groups, while very old pets (15+ years) are rare for both species.

Top 10 Breeds by Species

This chart highlights the ten most common breeds among registered pets in Geelong. Use the dropdown to switch between popular dog and cat breeds.

Dog ownership is dominated by Labradors, Jack Russells, Maltese/Shih Tzus and Border Collies. For cats, registrations are overwhelmingly Domestic Short-haired, Domestic and Domestic Long-haired, with pure-bred cats making up only a small share of total registrations.

After examining overall age profiles and breed popularity, the next chart combines these perspectives by showing how age varies within the ten most common breeds.

Age by Breed for Popular Dogs and Cats

This heatmap shows how pet ages are distributed across the ten most common breeds in Geelong. Darker cells indicate age groups with more registered pets. Use the dropdown below to switch between dogs and cats.

What the Heatmap Reveals

For dogs, the darkest cells sit between roughly 4 and 10 years across most popular breeds. Labradors, Jack Russells, Maltese/Shih Tzus and Border Collies all show a strong band in this middle-age range, with much lighter shading for very young (< 2 years) and older (> 12 years) pets.

For cats, Domestic Short Haired and Domestic cats dominate the grid, with their highest concentrations between about 3 and 8 years. The pedigree breeds (such as Burmese, Ragdoll, Birman and Siamese) have smaller clusters that are also mostly concentrated in these younger–middle ages. Beyond around 10 years, cells become noticeably paler for almost all cat breeds.

Overall, both heatmaps show that the most common breeds are mainly middle-aged pets. Dogs have a slightly broader spread into the later single-digit and early teen years, while cats, especially the pedigree breeds, appear more heavily weighted toward the younger and mid-life ranges.

Beyond age and breed, naming patterns offer another window into how households relate to their pets. The next visualisation highlights the most common names chosen by Geelong owners.

Most Common Pet Names in Geelong

This word cloud highlights the most frequently used pet names in the Geelong dataset. Larger names represent more registrations, revealing clear preferences for short, easily spoken names and familiar, human-like naming styles. The prominence of repeated favourites such as Bella, Charlie and Max also shows how strongly certain classic pet names dominate across households. Beyond which names are most popular, understanding the structure of those names reveals deeper patterns.

What the Name Patterns Suggest

The word cloud shows that Geelong pet owners favour a small set of very popular, easy-to-say names. Names like Bella, Charlie, Max and Molly appear much larger than the rest, meaning they are registered far more often than other names.

A strong human-name influence is visible: many top names (e.g., Oscar, Archie, Ruby, Lily) are common baby names in Australia. This suggests that pets are often treated as family members and named much like children.

Another clear trend is simplicity: most names are short (typically 4–6 letters) and easy to call out. There is also a noticeable mix of affectionate nicknames (Buddy, Lulu, Bear) and classic pet names (Rex, Shadow, Pepper).


Beyond which names are most popular, it is also useful to look at how those names are structured. The next charts explore patterns in name length and initial letters to show how owners tend to shape and choose their pets’ names.

How long are pet names?

While the word cloud highlights which individual names are most popular, the next two charts look at how those names are formed, their length and the initial letters owners tend to choose. This helps reveal broader naming patterns across dogs and cats in Geelong.

Name length distribution

The chart on the left compares how long pet names typically are for dogs and cats. Bars show how many registered pets have names of each length.

Most names fall between 4 and 7 characters, reflecting a strong preference for short, easy-to-call names. Dogs generally have slightly longer names than cats, which more often have very short, nickname-style names.

Name length patterns

The chart on the right shows which initial letters are most common. Names beginning with B, C and M are especially popular, while unusual initials such as Q, X and Z appear far less often.

Together with the word cloud, this suggests that most pets are given simple, human-like names that fit everyday use.


Together, these charts show that most pets are given simple, familiar names: short (4–7 characters), easy to pronounce, and often starting with common initials like B, C and M. Dogs tend to have slightly longer names than cats, while unusual or very long names remain rare. Together, these patterns reflect a strong preference for human-like, everyday names that are quick to call and widely recognisable.

Summary & Key Insights

Overall, the data shows that Geelong and Casey share many similarities in pet ownership, yet differ in density, species balance and registration detail. Dogs dominate registrations across both cities, with middle-aged pets being the most common and popular breeds showing clear age clusters. Naming patterns further highlight a strong preference for short, human-like names that are easy to call. Taken together, these insights reveal how geography, demographics and cultural preferences shape the way households choose, identify and care for their pets.
